A letter that I wrote to my state representatives. As per the expected, they simply ignored it. So now it's posted on my blog for everyone else to read.

## 

As a constituent and software developer, I am writing to urge you to introduce or support legislation to repeal California's Digital Age Assurance Act (AB 1043). This law requires that, beginning January 1, 2027, all operating systems require users to indicate their age at account setup. In addition, the operating system must make the user's age information available through a real-time application programming interface (API) to all websites or software applications that facilitate downloading third-party applications. The law is enforced under threat of a fine to the entity that develops the operating system, of up to $7,500 per child "affected" (it is unclear what is meant by affected).

This law is gravely unconstitutional. In Bernstein v. United States, the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals ruled that software source code is speech protected by the First Amendment. The compelled speech doctrine protects the right to remain silent and prohibits the government from punishing a person for refusing to articulate, advocate, or adhere to the government’s approved messages. Put plainly by Chief Justice John G. Roberts Jr., "this Court’s leading First Amendment precedents have established the principle that freedom of speech prohibits the government from telling people what they must say".

Senate Bill 2420, a bill similar to AB 1043, was attempted in Texas, but was blocked by a federal district court under the premise that "without proper guidance, the provision 'interferes with the right of free speech' in a way that is constitutionally unacceptable and must be enjoined". In that case, U.S. District Judge Robert Pitman stated "the Act is akin to a law that would require every bookstore to verify the age of every customer at the door and, for minors, require parental consent before the child or teen could enter and again when they try to purchase a book". The statements and decisions in that court case ought to apply equally to AB 1043.

Protecting minors from pornographic material is a noble goal that I support. However, websites and apps that distribute pornographic material are already required by law in California to ask users their age and reject non-adults. Not only is requiring the same of operating systems redundant, but operating systems do not distribute pornography and therefore ought not be compelled to collect a user's age. There is no reason why users that have no intent to visit “adult websites” should be legally required to input their birth date into their operating system. 

In addition to violating the first amendment, AB 1043 is a fourth amendment violation in terms of right to privacy. In practice, this law causes citizens of California to hand over the personally identifiable information (personally identifiable when combined with other data points) of their age bracket to every website or application they use. From a technical perspective, it is difficult for the operating system to limit this information to only websites and applications that download software. The law dictates the API to respond with the less precise information of the user's age as one of four brackets: under 13, between 13 and 16, between 16 and 18, and at least 18. However, there is no option for the API's response to reveal no information about the user's age.

Although this law was designed to protect children, I foresee it being used to harm them more effectively. Currently, websites and applications can only know a user's age if the user provides it directly to the service. AB 1043 would, in practice, allow websites and apps to know the age range of the user without the user directly providing their age to the service. The effect of this will be that media outlets such as social media are able to more accurately track how each age demographic interacts with types of content. This will then allow those media outlets to serve child users more targeted content in order to manipulate the user and keep them more engaged in the platform.

An additional concern, currently, how age verification is supposed to work is that a platform verifies a users age, and then immediately afterwards deletes the user's personal information. After AB 1043 goes into effect, operating systems will be required to hold onto the user's birth date, age, or both indefinitely. If a malicious software gains access to the operating system, it could steal the user's birth date and use that information to help bypass security questions that are asked when trying to access bank accounts and other sensitive accounts.

My last concern is that once California realizes that users can easily bypass this age check by lying about their birth date, California will enact even more invasive laws requiring operating systems to not only ask for the user's age, but verify the user's age using a government id or credit card. If this happens, you will need permission from the government to use a computer in your own home. Possibly, the California government would then move on to use this age verification platform to restrict children from accessing any platforms that the current administration deems to be “misinformation” or “fake news” depending on the current administration’s agenda.

If you have read this far, thank you.
